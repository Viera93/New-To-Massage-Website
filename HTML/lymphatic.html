Lymphatic Massage

Lymphatic Drainage is a therapeutic massage treatment. The massage uses very light pressure and long, gentle, rhytmic strokes to increase the flow of the lymph. This technique is supposed to help remove waste and toxins from the body.

Treatment usually takes place in a heated room, as warmth is an important factor in increasing lymphatic flow. You are also likely to be more relaxed if you are not cold.

Lymphatic system

The lymphatic system primarily consists of lymphatic vessels, which are similar to the veins and capillaries of the circulatory system. The vessels are connected to lymph nodes, where the lymph is filtered. The tonsils, adenoids, spleen and thymus are all part of the lymphatic system. The primary function of the Lymphatic system is to transport lymph, a fluid containing infection-fighting white blood cells, throughout the body. Unlike blood, which flows throughout the body in a continue loop, lumph flows in only one direction - upward toward neck. Lymphatic vessels connect to two subclavian veins, which are located on either sides of the neck near the collarbones, and the fluid re-enters the circulatory system.

The most common deseases of the Lymphatic system are enlargement of the lymph nodes (lymphadenopathy), swelling due to lymph node blockage (lymphedema) and cancers inolving the lymphatic systems. When bacteria are recognized in the lymph fluid, lymph nodes make more infection-fighting white blood cells, which can cause swelling. The swallen nodes can sometimes be felt in the neck, underarm and groin.

When you should avoid Lymphatic massage?

  • Thrombosis (can lead to free floating blood clots in the circulatory system)
  • Major heart problems
  • Malignant tumours
  • acute inflammation or infections (the germs could be spread in your body by performing the lymphatic drainage)
  • first three months of pregnancy
